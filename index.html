"I am not a woman who weeps in silence. I am fire. And fire does not ask permission."

— Kannagi, Cilappatikāram · c. 2nd century CE

The Cilappatikāram — one of Tamil literature's greatest epics — tells us of Kannagi, a woman wronged by a king's court. She did not petition. She did not compromise. She tore off her ankle-bracelet, struck the ground, and set Madurai ablaze with righteous truth.

🔥

The king had the power. The court had the process. But Kannagi had the facts — and the courage to present them without apology.

Two thousand years later, we sat in a play at Whitefield and watched her story unfold. Walking back, we realized: every woman at Lumberfi carries a piece of that fire.

Not the destruction. The clarity. The refusal to let bias masquerade as process. The insistence that what is right matters more than what is comfortable.

The question this Women's Day is not "are women good at tech?" — that's been answered. The question is: will AI inherit Kannagi's courage, or the king's blind court?

Breaking 1000 Years of Prejudice

T-shaped. M-shaped.
Kannagi-shaped.

For a thousand years, the world told women to stay narrow. One role. One room. One lane.

Kannagi refused. She was wife, witness, prosecutor, and force of nature — all at once. Multi-domain. Uncontainable.

That's exactly what AI needs now. Not specialists who only see their corner of the code. But T-shaped thinkers (deep expertise, broad empathy) and M-shaped builders (multiple domains, multiple truths) who can question what the model assumes and who it forgets.

A startup like Lumberfi is one of the rare places where young women get that platform — without being asked to shrink first. The prejudice of a thousand years doesn't break with a policy. It breaks when Niharika ensures every product ships without a single bug, Ayushi reviews the architecture, Bhuwaneshwari decides the AI product direction, and Anusha tells us what the market actually needs.

That's not diversity. That's how good AI gets built.
